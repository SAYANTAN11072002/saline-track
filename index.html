HYPER SALINE TRACT OF INDIAN SUNDARBAN

This study investigates the spatial mapping of hypersaline tracts within mangrove forest floors, where prolonged evaporation and limited freshwater inflow create extreme salinity conditions that inhibit vegetation. In mature mangrove swamps, tidal scouring without compensatory silting lowers interior floodplains, forming salt-encrusted flats and unvegetated pans. Such hypersaline areas develop in abandoned deltaic tidal swamps, supra-tidal pools, linear creek depressions, and elongate chutes along channel banks. To extract these tracts, the research employs a simulation-based method using remote sensing principles, specifically leveraging differences in red and near-infrared (NIR) reflectance. Dense vegetation strongly absorbs red light and reflects NIR, while bare hypersaline soil shows higher red reflectance and slower NIR increase, allowing indices like NDVI, RVI, and DVI to differentiate stressed or absent vegetation. The proposed approach enhances detection of hypersaline zones where even halophytes cannot survive, aiding ecological monitoring of degraded mangrove habitats.
